It was a bare garret-room, with a dim light burning at the farther end. There was another old woman watching by the bed; the parish apothecary’s apprentice was standing by the fire, making a toothpick out of a quill. “Cold night, Mrs. Corney,” said this young gentleman, as the matron entered. “Very cold, indeed, sir,” replied the mistress, in her most civil tones, and dropping a curtsey as she spoke. “You should get better coals out of your contractors,” said the apothecary’s deputy, breaking a lump on the top of the fire with the rusty poker; “these are not at all the sort of thing for a cold night.” “They’re the board’s choosing, sir,” returned the matron. “The least they could do, would be to keep us pretty warm: for our places are hard enough.” The conversation was here interrupted by a moan from the sick woman. “Oh!” said the young man, turning his

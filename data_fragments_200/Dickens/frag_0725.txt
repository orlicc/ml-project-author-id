Make him come home, there’s good people, or he’ll kill his dear mother and father, and break my heart!” “What the devil’s this?” said a man, bursting out of a beer-shop, with a white dog at his heels; “young Oliver! Come home to your poor mother, you young dog! Come home directly.” “I don’t belong to them. I don’t know them. Help! help!” cried Oliver, struggling in the man’s powerful grasp. “Help!” repeated the man. “Yes; I’ll help you, you young rascal! What books are these? You’ve been a stealing ’em, have you? Give ’em here.” With these words, the man tore the volumes from his grasp, and struck him on the head. “That’s right!” cried a looker-on, from a garret-window. “That’s the only way of bringing him to his senses!” “To be sure!” cried a sleepy-faced carpenter, casting an approving look at the garret-window. “It’ll do him good!” said the two women. “And he shall have it, too!” rejoined
